Chapter 0 · Orientation

Before we begin — a question for you.

Before we teach you anything about electricity, we want to ask you one thing. Take a moment. Be honest with yourself.

What do you think an electrician actually does all day?

Write it down, say it out loud, or just think about it. By the time you finish this course, your answer will be completely different — and that's exactly the point.

So what is this trade, really?

Most people picture an electrician as someone who fixes a blown fuse or replaces an outlet. That's like saying a surgeon is someone who puts on band-aids. It's not wrong — it's just a tiny sliver of a much bigger world.

Electricians are the people who make buildings come alive. Before a single wall goes up, before a floor is poured, before the furniture arrives — electricians are already there, laying the invisible infrastructure that will power everything inside for the next 50 years. They read blueprints, bend conduit through walls and ceilings, pull hundreds of feet of wire through tight spaces, install panels that distribute power throughout entire buildings, and test every circuit to make sure it's safe.

They work in homes, hospitals, schools, factories, skyscrapers, and data centers. They install solar panels and EV charging stations. They wire emergency systems that keep hospitals running when the grid fails. They are, in every meaningful sense, the people who keep the modern world running.

"The electrician who shows up early, asks questions, and keeps learning — that person will never be out of work."

Why this course exists

Darco Inc created this course because the path into the electrical trade is unnecessarily confusing for beginners. This course starts from absolute zero. It uses plain language. It tells you not just what to do but why — because understanding the why is what separates someone who can follow instructions from someone who can actually think on the job.

Chapter 1 · The Trade

The world you're about to enter.

Before you study a single wire, you need to understand what you're actually walking into — and why it's one of the best career decisions you can make in 2025.

The numbers don't lie

The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 11% growth in electrician employment through 2030 — more than double the average for all occupations. That's roughly 81,000 job openings every single year. The median annual wage for electricians in 2024 was $62,350, with the top 10% earning over $106,000. Master electricians who own their own businesses frequently earn $150,000–$200,000 per year.

You don't need a four-year degree. No student debt. No years of classroom study before you earn your first dollar. The electrical trade is one of the few remaining careers in America where you can start earning real money within weeks of your first day of study.

Your career arc — what to expect

H

Helper / Electrician's assistant

Months 1–12

This is where you start. No license needed. No experience required.

You pull wire, mount conduit, stage materials, and watch everything. Pay: $17–$22/hr. Day one of your experience clock — every hour counts toward your future license.

A

Apprentice-level employee

Years 1–3

You're trusted with real work. You ask fewer questions because you know more answers.

Handle installations under supervision, read blueprints, bend conduit, wire outlets and switches. Pay: $22–$35/hr.

J

Working electrician / Journeyman-level

Years 3–6

You work independently. You supervise newer helpers.

The backbone of any crew. Pay: $35–$55/hr in most markets, higher in major cities.

M

Master electrician / License holder

Years 6–10+

The license changes everything. You can pull permits, run projects, start a business.

Full independence, ability to bid contracts, run your own operation. Pay: $60–$100+/hr or $100K–$200K/yr running your own company.

💡 The secret most beginners don't know: Non-union electrical experience counts toward your license just as much as union experience — as long as you're working under a licensed master electrician. The direct-hire path and the union path lead to the exact same license.

Chapter 2 · Safety

Safety isn't a rule.
It's a culture.

Every experienced electrician will tell you the same thing: the most important skill in this trade isn't knowing how to wire a panel. It's knowing how to come home alive every day.

Why this chapter comes first

Electricity is invisible. You can't see it, smell it, or hear it in most cases. The amount flowing through a standard household circuit — 120 volts — can stop your heart. There are about 45,000 electrical fires each year in the United States, causing 440 deaths, 1,250 injuries, and over a billion dollars in property damage. Almost all of them are preventable.

The four rules that protect you every day

Rule 1 — Always de-energize

  • Turn off the circuit breaker before any work
  • Lock it out and tag it — Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
  • Test with a non-contact voltage tester before touching anything
  • Test. Every. Time.

Rule 2 — Use your PPE

  • Safety glasses — always when working overhead or drilling
  • Insulated gloves — when working near live components
  • Flame-resistant (FR) clothing — required in arc flash environments
  • Hard-soled, non-conductive boots

Rule 3 — Respect arc flash

  • Arc flash is an explosive release of electrical energy — it can reach 35,000°F
  • It happens in milliseconds — faster than you can react
  • Never work on energized panels without proper PPE and training

Rule 4 — Avoid moisture always

  • Water and electricity are a lethal combination
  • Never work with wet hands or in wet environments
  • GFCI protection is required near water sources — understand why
⚠️ The most dangerous words in the trade: "It should be off." Should be is not good enough. Test every time, without exception. The majority of electrical injuries happen when someone assumed a circuit was de-energized without verifying it.

Chapter 4 · Tools

The tools of the trade.

An electrician is only as good as their tools — and knowing every tool by name and function before you step on a job site immediately marks you as serious.

🛒 What to buy first: A quality voltage tester (~$20), a multimeter (~$30–60), Klein Tools lineman's pliers, and a wire stripper. These four items cover most of what a helper needs on day one and cost under $150 total.

Hand tools — your everyday carry

🔧

Lineman's pliers

The signature tool of the trade. Cuts, bends, grips, and twists wire. Every electrician carries these. Klein Tools is the industry standard.

🔧

Needle-nose pliers

For working in tight spaces, forming wire hooks for screw terminals, and grabbing things in boxes where your fingers won't fit.

✂️

Wire strippers

Removes insulation from wire ends without damaging the conductor. Has calibrated notches for different wire gauges. Learn the gauges.

🔩

Screwdrivers (insulated)

Phillips and flathead, multiple sizes. Must be properly insulated (rated 1000V). Never use a standard hardware store screwdriver around electrical work.

🔍

Non-contact voltage tester

Your most important safety tool. Detects voltage without touching the wire. Beeps and lights up if live. Use it before touching anything, every time.

📊

Multimeter

Measures voltage, current, and resistance. Essential for troubleshooting. Learn to use this early — it tells you what's happening in any circuit.

🎣

Fish tape

Thin, flexible steel tape used to pull wire through conduit or walls. Comes in 50–100 foot lengths. Essential for running wire through finished spaces.

🔨

Conduit bender

Bends EMT and rigid conduit to precise angles. One of the most skill-dependent tools in the trade. Learning to bend clean 90s will make you stand out.

Chapter 5 · How Electricity Works

Understanding what you can't see.

Every electrician works with something invisible. Understanding what electricity actually is — not just how to wire things — is what separates a tradesperson from a technician.

The plumbing analogy

Think about your home's plumbing system. Water enters under pressure, flows through pipes, does its work, and exits through the drain. Electrical current works almost identically.

In plumbing, pressure is measured in PSI. In electricity, pressure is called voltage (volts — V). Flow rate in plumbing is gallons per minute. In electricity, flow is called current (amperes — A). A narrow pipe restricts flow. In electricity, that restriction is called resistance (ohms — Ω).

Ohm's Law — V = I × R

V = I × R  ·  I = V ÷ R  ·  R = V ÷ I

Voltage = Current × Resistance · Rearrange to solve for any unknown

Rule 1 — Electricity needs a complete path

  • Current must flow in a closed loop — from source, through the load, and back
  • Break the loop anywhere and current stops — that's what a switch does

Rule 2 — Electricity always does work

  • Current converts energy to something else — light, heat, motion
  • A light bulb converts it to light and heat; a motor converts it to motion

Rule 3 — Electricity flows high to low

  • Current always flows from higher voltage toward lower voltage
  • This explains why grounding works the way it does

Rule 4 — Electricity finds the easiest path

  • Given multiple paths, current takes the one with least resistance
  • This is why a short circuit is dangerous — it bypasses the load entirely

Chapter 6 · Wire & Cable

The material that carries everything.

Wire is to an electrician what lumber is to a carpenter. You need to know every type, every size, every color code, and when each one is used — cold, without looking it up.

Wire sizing — the AWG system

Wire is sized using the American Wire Gauge (AWG) system. The counterintuitive part: the higher the number, the thinner the wire. A 14-gauge wire is thinner than a 10-gauge wire. Always match wire gauge to the circuit's amperage.

Chapter 7 · Conduit & Raceways

Protecting wire is half the job.

Conduit is the protective tubing that wire travels through. Understanding it — and learning to bend it well — is one of the fastest ways to stand out as an apprentice.

⚙️

EMT — Electrical Metallic Tubing

The most common conduit in commercial work. Thin-wall steel, easy to bend, cost-effective. Used in dry indoor locations.

🔩

Rigid Metal Conduit (RMC)

Heavy-duty thick-wall steel. Used outdoors, in wet locations, where maximum physical protection is needed.

🟦

PVC Conduit

Gray plastic. Lightweight, corrosion-resistant, inexpensive. Used underground and in wet locations.

🌀

Flexible Metal Conduit (FMC)

Flexible spiral metal armor — used to connect rigid conduit runs to equipment that vibrates (motors, HVAC units).

The 5 essential bends every apprentice must master

  • Stub-up (90° bend) — the most common bend. Brings conduit up from a floor at 90 degrees. Master this one first.
  • Forward 90° — measured from the end of the pipe with a takeoff calculation (5" for ½", 6" for ¾", 8" for 1")
  • Back 90° — uses the star mark on the bender, no takeoff needed. Simple one-mark bend.
  • Offset bend — two bends in opposite directions to clear an obstacle. Multiply obstacle height × 2 for 30° bends.
  • Box offset — tiny offset at end of conduit run to align flush with a box knockout. No measuring needed — feel it.
  • Kick — single bend to change height. Only half an offset — the easiest and fastest bend in the trade.

Chapter 8 · Boxes, Switches & Outlets

Where the wire meets the world.

Electrical boxes, switches, and receptacles are where an invisible wire system becomes something people actually use. This is the most hands-on chapter in the course.

Receptacles — understanding the basics

A lot of people call these "plugs" — but a plug is the thing you stick in. The wall device that receives it is a receptacle. This distinction matters on the job and in interviews.

Switch types every beginner must know

  • Single-pole switch — controls a light from one location. Two terminals. Most common switch in any home.
  • 3-way switch — controls a light from two locations. Three terminals including a "common."
  • 4-way switch — used between two 3-way switches for three or more control locations.
  • Dimmer switch — controls light level. Must be matched to fixture type.

Receptacle types to know

  • Duplex receptacle (15A) — standard two-outlet on a 15-amp/14 AWG circuit. Most common.
  • 20-amp receptacle — T-shaped neutral slot. Required on 20-amp circuits.
  • GFCI receptacle — TEST and RESET buttons. Required within 6 feet of water sources per NEC.
  • AFCI receptacle — Arc Fault protection. Required in bedrooms and living spaces per 2020 NEC.
ℹ️ GFCI vs AFCI — know the difference: GFCI protects people from electrocution (detects current leaking to ground). AFCI protects property from fire (detects arcing). They solve different problems. The NEC specifies exactly where each is required.
📋 NEC 2023 update: GFCI protection now required for hardwired appliances including electric ranges, ovens, dryers, and microwave ovens per Section 210.8(D) — not just receptacles near water.

Chapter 9 · Lighting & Panels

Lighting up rooms — and the brain of every building.

Lighting circuits and electrical panels are two of the most visible, most inspected, and most important parts of any electrical installation.

The electrical panel — what's inside

The main electrical panel (load center, breaker box, service panel) is where all electrical circuits in a building originate. Understanding what's inside is fundamental knowledge every employer expects.

Inside a residential electrical panel

  • Bus bars (A-phase & B-phase) — the metal bars that give the panel its amperage rating. A 200A panel means each bus bar can carry 200A continuously.
  • Main breaker — disconnects all branch circuits. Does NOT de-energize the service entrance wires at the top — those are always live.
  • Neutral bus bar — isolated with plastic. White wires land here. At zero volts when functioning normally.
  • Ground bus bar — bare copper equipment grounds land here. Bonded to neutral only at the main service panel — never at subpanels.
  • Main bonding jumper (green screw) — only installed at the service panel. This is one of the most inspected items.
  • Single-pole breakers — 15A and 20A for lighting and outlet circuits. Connect to one bus bar, carry 120V.
  • Double-pole breakers — 240V circuits for dryers, ranges, A/C, water heaters. Connect to both bus bars.
  • GFCI/AFCI breakers — protect entire circuits rather than individual devices.
⚠️ The service entrance wires are ALWAYS live. The main breaker disconnects branch circuits — not the wires feeding into the panel from the utility. Those wires carry lethal voltage 24/7. Only the utility company can de-energize them.

Chapter 10 · Reading Plans & Codes

The language every electrician must speak.

Blueprint reading and code knowledge are what separate someone who follows instructions from someone who can think independently on a job site.

Essential electrical symbols to memorize

  • Circle + 2 lines — duplex receptacle (standard outlet)
  • Circle + 3 lines — 240V receptacle
  • Circle + GFCI — GFCI protected receptacle
  • S — single-pole switch
  • S3 — 3-way switch
  • S4 — 4-way switch
  • SD — dimmer switch
  • Square with circle inside — recessed can light
  • Circle with X — ceiling light fixture
  • SD — smoke detector
  • J (in box) — junction box
  • KP — keypad (lighting control)
  • OS — occupancy sensor

Key NEC articles every beginner should know

  • Article 100 — Definitions. The dictionary of the NEC.
  • Article 110 — General requirements for all installations.
  • Article 200 — Use and identification of grounded conductors.
  • Article 210 — Branch circuits, outlet spacing, room requirements.
  • Article 230 — Services — how power enters a building.
  • Article 250 — Grounding and bonding. Most inspected article.
  • Article 300 — Wiring methods — how wire must be installed.
  • Article 310 — Conductors, ampacity tables, wire types.
  • Article 334 — NM cable (Romex) installation requirements.
📖 You don't need to memorize the NEC. You need to know how to use it — how it's organized, where to look things up, and which articles govern which types of work. Experienced electricians carry a code book on every job site and know how to find the answer fast.

Chapter 11 · Getting Hired

Everything you've learned — now put it to work.

If you've worked through this course, you already know more than most people who apply for helper positions. Now let's make sure you get the job.

What employers are actually looking for

Here's the honest truth about hiring at the helper level: most employers are not looking for someone who knows everything. They're looking for someone who is safe, reliable, and teachable. Technical knowledge is a bonus at this stage. Character is the requirement.

Must have

OSHA 10 Card

The first thing most contractors check. Get it before you apply to anything.

Must have

Reliable transportation

Job sites move and change. A valid driver's license and reliable transportation are frequently listed requirements.

Strong advantage

Basic theory knowledge

Being able to explain Ohm's Law and name wire types in an interview immediately sets you apart.

Strong advantage

Certificates on your resume

Alison and SkillCat certificates show initiative. Employers notice the person who started learning before they got hired.

Big plus

Physical readiness

This is physical work — lifting, climbing, kneeling, working in tight spaces. Mention it. Employers appreciate it.

Most important

Genuine enthusiasm

Contractors hire people they want to spend 8 hours a day with. Someone excited about the trade is worth more than any certificate.

Job boards — search these terms

  • Indeed.com ↗ — "electrician helper" or "electrical apprentice no experience"
  • ZipRecruiter.com ↗ — "electrician helper no experience"
  • Craigslist — Skilled Trades ↗ — many small contractors post here exclusively; check daily
  • LinkedIn ↗ — good for commercial contractors and larger companies

Direct outreach — often more effective

  • Search Google Maps for "electrical contractor [your city]" and call directly
  • Many small contractors never post jobs online — they hire by phone and word of mouth
  • Walk into electrical supply houses — contractors come in daily
  • Tell everyone in your network — the trades run heavily on personal referrals

Words that work in electrician interviews

  • Be honest: "I have no field experience yet, but I've completed this course, earned my OSHA 10 card, and I'm serious about building a career in this trade."
  • Show what you know: Be ready to explain Ohm's Law, name wire gauges for 15 and 20-amp circuits, and describe what a GFCI does.
  • Emphasize attitude: "I show up early, I'm physically fit, I take direction well, and I ask questions when I'm unsure."
  • Ask smart questions: "What types of projects does your company mostly work on?" — shows you understand the trade has different sectors.
🎯 Target small to mid-size non-union residential and commercial contractors. They train as they go, give you real responsibility faster, and are much more likely to hire someone with no experience. A company with 5–20 employees is your best starting point.

Once you're hired — the two things that matter most

1. Keep a job diary. Every day, write down what you worked on, what systems you touched, and what you learned. This is your legal proof of experience for your eventual license application. Start it on day one and never stop.

2. Keep learning. Continue watching the recommended YouTube channels. Start listening to the Electrician U podcast on your commute. The electricians who advance fastest are the ones who study at home what they saw on the job that day.

You're ready.

You now know more about the electrical trade than the vast majority of people who walk into interviews every day. Go get the job. The work starts now.
